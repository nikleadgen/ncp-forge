# Deep Research Skill

This skill encodes world-class research methodology for knowledge
synthesis and skill building. It draws from four traditions:

1. **Systematic Review Methodology** (Cochrane/academic) — rigor,
   breadth, bias control
2. **Munger/Parrish Mental Models** — multidisciplinary synthesis,
   latticework thinking
3. **Tetlock Superforecasting** — epistemic humility, calibration,
   updating beliefs
4. **Steelmanning** — actively seeking the strongest counter-arguments

The goal is not just to find information. The goal is to find the
**best available thinking**, stress-test it against opposing views,
synthesize it across disciplines, and encode only what survives.

---

## PHASE 1 — Define the Research Question Precisely

The most common research failure is starting with a vague question and
finding vague answers. Before the first search, define:

**1. The specific question being answered**
Not: "Research brand strategy"
Yes: "What are the best-validated frameworks for positioning a new
consumer brand in a crowded market where the primary differentiator is
consumer advocacy and trust?"

**2. The type of knowledge needed**
- **Conceptual** — what is this, how does it work, what are the
  frameworks?
- **Empirical** — what does the evidence show actually works?
- **Practitioner** — who has done this successfully and what did they
  learn?
- **Contrarian** — who thinks the mainstream view is wrong, and why?

**3. The knowledge cutoff problem**
Is this a domain where knowledge changes rapidly (SEO, AI, technology)
or slowly (human psychology, brand building, persuasion)? Fast-changing
domains require source recency checks. Slow-changing domains can use
older seminal sources.

**4. The "minimum viable certainty" threshold**
What level of confidence is needed before encoding this into a skill?
- High-stakes (business decisions, technical architecture) → higher
  threshold
- Lower-stakes (style preferences, content formats) → lower threshold

## PHASE 2 — Source Discovery Protocol

### 2A: The Five Source Tiers

Never rely on a single tier. World-class research triangulates across
at least three tiers.

**Tier 1 — Primary Practitioners (Highest Value)**
People who have actually done the thing at world-class level and
documented their methods.

How to find them:
- Who is consistently cited by other credible practitioners?
- Who has demonstrated results (books, companies built, campaigns run)?
- Who challenges the mainstream view with evidence from their own
  experience?

Warning: High follower count ≠ top practitioner. Find who the
practitioners follow and cite, not who the general audience follows.

**Tier 2 — Synthesizers and Meta-Researchers (High Value)**
People who have read extensively across a domain and synthesized the
best thinking. Valuable for breadth and cross-domain pattern
recognition.

Warning: Synthesizers are only as good as their sources. Always check
what they're citing.

**Tier 3 — Academic Research (High Value for Foundational Claims)**
Peer-reviewed research, systematic reviews, meta-analyses. Most reliable
for empirical claims. Slowest to update.

Source quality check: Peer-reviewed > conference paper > working paper
> blog post with citations > blog post without citations.

**Tier 4 — Case Studies and Documented Examples (Medium Value)**
Real-world applications with documented outcomes. More reliable than
theory, less reliable than controlled research. Multiple case studies
pointing the same direction >> single case study.

**Tier 5 — Secondary Summaries (Low Value, High Availability)**
Blog posts summarizing books, YouTube videos explaining frameworks,
listicles. Where most AI research stops. **This is not deep research.**

Correct use: Discovery only — finding the names of primary sources to
go find. Never cite a summary as evidence.

### 2B: The Source Discovery Process

**Step 1: Cast Wide First**
3–5 broad searches across different framings of the question. Goal:
identify 5–10 named practitioners/researchers consistently cited.

**Step 2: Go Deep on the Best Sources**
For each: their primary methodology, specific claims, evidence cited,
their influences, their critics.

**Step 3: Follow the Citation Chain**
Stop when you reach original empirical research, the person who first
articulated the idea, or an uncited claim (flag this).

**Step 4: Look for Disagreement**
Actively search for practitioners who disagree with the mainstream
view. "Why [framework] doesn't work" / "criticism of [framework]" /
"when [approach] fails"

Not to reject good frameworks — to find the boundary conditions.

## PHASE 3 — Source Evaluation Protocol

Every source evaluated on five dimensions:

**1. Expertise** — Has the person actually done this? Direct
high-stakes experience? Verifiable track record? Cited by other
credible practitioners?

**2. Evidence** — Specific evidence for specific claims? Primary or
secondary? Can the evidence be checked? Representative or
cherry-picked?

**3. Recency** — For fast-changing domains, when was this published?
Has context changed since? For slow-changing domains, older seminal
works often more reliable than recent summaries.

**4. Independence** — Conflicts of interest? Doesn't automatically
invalidate, but read with more scrutiny.

**5. Cross-Validation** — Claim confirmed by three independent sources
from different traditions is much more reliable than three sources in
the same echo chamber.

## PHASE 4 — Anti-Bias Protocol

The single biggest research failure is confirmation bias. This phase is
mandatory.

### 4A: The Steelman Requirement

For every major framework being encoded:

1. **Find the strongest critics.** What is their best argument?
2. **Steelman the criticism.** Restate it stronger than the critic
   stated it. What would have to be true for the criticism to be
   completely correct?
3. **Test the framework against the criticism.** Does it survive the
   strongest version? Encode with awareness of boundary conditions. If
   not, revise or reject.
4. **Document the boundary conditions.** Every framework has conditions
   under which it works and doesn't. Encode both.

### 4B: The "What's Missing?" Check

Before finalizing, ask explicitly:
- What major viewpoints have I NOT found yet?
- What disciplines haven't I consulted?
- What would a skeptic say is missing?
- What's the failure mode of the conclusions I'm drawing?

### 4C: The "Why Now" Currency Check

For fast-moving fields: search "[framework] outdated" / "[framework] no
longer works" / "beyond [framework]" / "problems with [framework] in
[current year]"

## PHASE 5 — Synthesis Protocol

### 5A: Munger's Latticework Test

Before encoding, test:
- Do findings from different sources connect and reinforce?
- Do different frameworks point to the same underlying principles?
- Can you explain WHY each framework works, not just THAT it works?
- If two sources contradict, do you understand why — and which is right
  for which context?

If the research is just a list of separate frameworks that don't
connect, synthesis is incomplete. Keep going.

### 5B: The Feynman Explanation Test

Explain each framework's core insight in plain language without jargon.
If you can't, synthesis is superficial.

### 5C: The "Big Idea" Extraction

For each domain researched, identify:
- The 2–3 most important underlying principles (not frameworks —
  principles)
- The single most important insight that survives the steelman test
- The boundary conditions

## PHASE 6 — Research Documentation Standard

For each major claim or framework encoded, document:

```
CLAIM: [The specific claim being encoded]
SOURCE: [Who makes this claim — tier, credentials]
EVIDENCE: [What grounds the claim]
COUNTER: [Strongest criticism and how it was addressed]
BOUNDARY: [When this applies and when it doesn't]
RECENCY: [When was this established, is it still current]
CONFIDENCE: [High / Medium / Low — and why]
```

Claims marked Low Confidence get flagged visibly in the skill:
`⚠️ LOW CONFIDENCE — validate with real-world examples before relying on this`

## PHASE 7 — Research Checklist (Run Before Finalizing)

**Breadth:**
- [ ] Sources from at least 3 of the 5 tiers?
- [ ] At least 3 independent primary practitioners/researchers?
- [ ] At least 2 different disciplines consulted?
- [ ] Sources that disagree with each other?

**Depth:**
- [ ] Each major framework: origin + evidence behind it known?
- [ ] Citation chains followed to primary sources?
- [ ] Each principle explainable in plain language without jargon?
- [ ] Boundary conditions documented?

**Bias:**
- [ ] Actively searched for critics of each major framework?
- [ ] Steelmanned the strongest criticism?
- [ ] Searched for "why [framework] doesn't work" variations?
- [ ] Would a disagreeing reader find the research fair?

**Recency** (fast-moving fields):
- [ ] Sources current enough for the domain?
- [ ] Checked practitioners still use these approaches?
- [ ] Searched for recent shifts in thinking?

**Synthesis:**
- [ ] Findings from different sources connect and reinforce?
- [ ] 2–3 underlying principles articulable (not just frameworks)?
- [ ] Latticework, not a list?

**Documentation:**
- [ ] Confidence levels assigned to major claims?
- [ ] Boundary conditions documented for each framework?

If ANY item is unchecked, research is not complete. Do not encode into
a skill.

## PHASE 8 — Minimum Research Standard by Skill Type

**Technical skills (code architecture, performance, infrastructure):**
- Mandatory: Official documentation from the primary source
- Mandatory: Current practitioner consensus (recency critical)
- Mandatory: Edge cases and common failure modes
- Mandatory: What changed in last 12–24 months
- Searches required: minimum 8–12

**Strategic/business skills (brand, positioning, ICP):**
- Mandatory: Primary practitioner methodology (books, courses,
  documented frameworks)
- Mandatory: Academic validation where it exists
- Mandatory: Steelmanned criticism of each major framework
- Mandatory: Cross-domain validation
- Searches required: minimum 6–10

**Creative skills (writing, content, copy):**
- Mandatory: Examples of world-class execution
- Mandatory: Underlying principles (not just "do this" but "why")
- Mandatory: Common failure modes
- Searches required: minimum 5–8

**Process skills (workflows, checklists, systems):**
- Mandatory: Validation that the process produces the intended outcome
- Mandatory: Edge cases and failure modes
- Mandatory: Simplest effective version (Occam's razor)
- Searches required: minimum 4–6

## Application Note

This skill is the meta-skill. Every time a new skill is being built,
run this skill first. The research phase should take as long as — often
longer than — the skill writing phase.

**Signs research is insufficient:**
- Only sources that agree with each other
- All sources from the same tradition or discipline
- Can't articulate what would make you wrong
- Fewer searches than the minimum for the skill type
- Haven't found a single critic

**Signs research is sufficient:**
- Sources from different traditions reach similar conclusions
- You can articulate the strongest criticism AND why the framework
  survives it
- You know exactly where the framework applies and doesn't
- You can explain the underlying principle without jargon
- You found something that surprised you or changed your initial
  assumption
