# The Science Behind Forge

Why your plan is built the way it is — and the research it stands on. Every number the app
prescribes traces back to something here. This was assembled from peer-reviewed exercise
physiology and S&C literature, then red-teamed by an independent panel before it shaped the plan.

> **Not medical advice.** This is a training rationale for a healthy adult. Respect pain, fuel and
> sleep well, and get cleared by a professional if anything hurts beyond normal training soreness.

---

## 1. What the New Christendom Press Games actually demand

You are not training to be "gym strong." You're training to win a **hybrid** contest that taxes five
distinct physical qualities — and the brutal part is they partly fight each other:

| Event | Primary quality | Trained by |
|---|---|---|
| **1RM Deadlift** (qualifier) | Maximal strength | Heavy, low-rep barbell pulling, peaked |
| **"Cindy"** 20-min AMRAP (qualifier) | Muscular + aerobic endurance | Pull-up/push-up capacity, pacing, engine |
| **Sandbag Ladder** (finals) | Power-endurance + grip | Explosive shouldering, heavy carries |
| **1-Mile Race** (finals) | Aerobic power + running economy | Zone-2 base → VO2max intervals |
| **Field Metcon** (finals) | Lactate tolerance + speed | Glycolytic intervals, sprint finishes |

Because every event scores 60 for 1st and −2 per place, **no event can be punted** — but the qualifier
is a hard top-30 gate, so the deadlift and Cindy are the absolute priority until you advance, then
you pivot hard to the three finals events. The biggest marginal points come from improving your
*weakest* finals event.

## 2. Concurrent training & the interference effect

Training strength and endurance together can blunt strength/power gains — the "interference effect"
first shown by [Hickson (1980)](https://www.barbellmedicine.com/blog/concurrent-training-and-the-interference-effect/).
But modern evidence reframes it as a **dose-and-timing problem, not a reason to avoid cardio**: the
meta-analytic hit to maximal strength and hypertrophy is near zero when programmed well
([Schumann et al. 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8891239/);
[Wilson et al. 2012 meta-analysis](https://journals.lww.com/nsca-jscr/Fulltext/2012/08000/Concurrent_Training__A_Meta_Analysis_Examining.35.aspx)).

What the research says to do — and what Forge does:

- **Running interferes more than cycling** (eccentric muscle damage). Forge uses easy running and
  low-impact options (bike/ruck/incline walk) for *base* work to spare the legs, and reserves real
  running for mile economy and metcon specificity.
- **Never stack a hard run in the hours before a heavy deadlift/squat** — the single worst
  configuration (acute AMPK suppression of mTOR + leg damage). Every block's `layoutNote` separates
  them by a day.
- **Cap hard endurance at ~2–3 quality sessions/week** during heavy strength blocks; layer unlimited
  easy Zone-2. Duration is the strongest interference predictor.
- **Power is the fragile quality** — the one outcome with reliable interference (SMD ≈ −0.28) and the
  shortest shelf-life. It must be trained *fresh* and protected near competition
  ([Wilson et al. 2012](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/51719597_Concurrent_Training_A_Meta-Analysis_Examining_Interference_of_Aerobic_and_Resistance_Exercises);
  [Baar, GSSI SSE #136](https://www.gssiweb.org/sports-science-exchange/article/sse-136-using-nutrition-and-molecular-biology-to-maximize-concurrent-training)).
- **Maintenance is cheap.** Strength holds on as little as 1–2 heavy, low-volume sessions/week
  ([Nuzzo et al. 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11127831/);
  [Androulakis-Korakakis et al., minimum effective dose for 1RM](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/337716488_The_Minimum_Effective_Training_Dose_Required_to_Increase_1RM_Strength_in_Resistance-Trained_Men_A_Systematic_Review_and_Meta-Analysis)).
  This is why Forge "parks" qualities you've already built and spends fresh energy on the priority.

## 3. Periodization, residuals & the double-peak

Forge is **block periodization** ([Issurin review](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Vladimir-Issurin/publication/5638447_Block_periodization_versus_traditional_training_theory_A_review/links/59a4f8074585157031179b96/Block-periodization-versus-traditional-training-theory-A-review.pdf);
[Stronger by Science: what the data say](https://www.strongerbyscience.com/periodization-data/)).
The organizing rule is **residual training effects** — how long an adaptation lasts after you stop
emphasizing it ([Issurin's residuals](http://rudytapalla.blogspot.com/2011/12/residual-training-effects.html)):

- **Long residual (~30 days):** max strength, aerobic base → build these *early*, then maintain.
- **Short residual (~5–18 days):** power, glycolytic/lactic endurance, speed → sharpen these *late*,
  close to competition.

Place qualities **inversely to their decay rate**, and you can peak everything for one day. The
52-week sequence:

1. **Foundation** (build tissue + aerobic base) → 2. **Hypertrophy/Work Capacity** →
3. **Aerobic Engine** (polarized base; strength to maintenance) → 4. **Max Strength** →
5. **Power, VO2max & Metcon** → 6. **Qualifier Peak** → 7. **Bridge → Finals**.

**Linear early, undulating later.** The first ~16 weeks run as simple progression (novice gains, low
complexity); once you're trained, the week becomes daily-undulating (heavy / power / rep days), which
gives a modest 1RM edge at equal volume
([Traditional vs. Undulating meta-analysis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/311452712_Traditional_vs_Undulating_Periodization_in_the_Context_of_Muscular_Strength_and_Hypertrophy_A_Meta-Analysis)).

### The crux: the ~5-week qualifier → finals gap

A long taper across that gap would **de-train the finals events** (power and glycolytic endurance fade
in days). So Forge treats the gap as a **mini block-periodization cycle, not a rest**: recover &
re-stimulate → overload the fast-decaying finals qualities (sandbag power, mile VO2, field metcon) →
short realization taper. The deadlift coasts on its ~30-day residual with just 1–2 heavy primers/week
— re-maxing it late would only steal recovery from the events that actually need sharpening.

### Tapering correctly

Tapers work by **cutting volume while maintaining intensity/specificity** — expect ~3% performance bump
([Bosquet et al. meta-analysis](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Effects-of-tapering-on-performance:-a-Bosquet-Montpetit/a41517ab5fa06b92568b861e2b1aa32b3003d214);
[Travis et al., peaking for powerlifting](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7552788/)).
For the deadlift: cut volume ~30–50%, hold intensity ≥85% until the final days, pull it 2–7 days out.
A controlled pre-taper *overload* triggers the supercompensation — but stop short of true functional
overreaching, which blunts the rebound
([Aubry et al.](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/259990600_Functional_Overreaching_The_Key_to_Peak_Performance_during_the_Taper)).

## 4. Autoregulation — how the app adjusts to you

Forge adjusts at four time-scales, exactly as you asked. Each is a published method.

**Set-to-set — RPE / RIR.** Loads are prescribed as *reps-in-reserve*, and the app nudges the next
set if your reported effort misses the target. RIR-based RPE is the validated autoregulation standard
([Zourdos et al. 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26049792/);
[Helms et al., applying the RIR scale](https://journals.lww.com/nsca-scj/fulltext/2016/08000/application_of_the_repetitions_in_reserve_based.10.aspx)).
Caveat baked into the plan: novices under-predict reps-to-failure, so early targets stay in the
accurate 1–3 RIR band and re-calibrate over time.

**Session — daily readiness.** Your 5-tap check (sleep, freshness, energy, calm, drive) scales load
and volume. Subjective wellness tracks training response better than most objective tools
([Saw et al., systematic review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26423706/)). Low readiness
auto-eases hard intervals to steady work and trims a set.

**Week — Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio.** Forge computes a 7-day vs 28-day session-load ratio as a
*trend brake*. The classic 0.8–1.3 "sweet spot" is a useful heuristic but **not a causal law** — it
has real statistical flaws, so Forge uses it to flag spikes, never as a sole veto
([Science for Sport](https://www.scienceforsport.com/acutechronic-workload-ratio/);
[Impellizzeri et al., pitfalls](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32502973/)).

**Cycle — your real numbers.** Every logged top set updates the working maxes that drive future loads
(estimated via the reps-RIR → %1RM relationship), so the plan literally gets heavier as you get
stronger. Deloads are proactive (every ~4 weeks early, then 5–6) **and** reactive — triggered by a
performance drop, sustained low readiness, or lingering pain
([Bell et al., a practical approach to deloading](https://shura.shu.ac.uk/35313/3/Bell-APracticalApproach(AM).pdf)).

## 5. Injury prevention for a detrained start (the #1 threat to the whole year)

Muscle and the cardiovascular system rebound in **weeks**; tendons and bone adapt over **months**. A
returning athlete *feels* ready for heavy pulls and sprint repeats long before the tissue is — the
classic path to patellar/Achilles tendinopathy and bone stress injury.

- **Tendon insurance from week 1:** heavy-slow resistance and isometric holds drive collagen synthesis
  and stiffness ([Baar on tendons](https://simplifaster.com/articles/tendons-mechanical-properties-training-athletes/);
  [Kjaer et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19706001/)).
- **Run-walk ramp, distance before speed:** progress weekly running ≤10% (cap 15%) with a down-week
  every 4th; velocity raises bone-stress-injury risk faster than mileage, so speed waits until you're
  6–8 weeks pain-free ([BSI return-to-run](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11393297/);
  [JOSPT BSI prevention](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2014.5334)). The 10% rule is a
  heuristic, not a guarantee — it's paired with the readiness check.
- **Sleep is a load variable:** chronic <8 h roughly doubles injury risk and cuts muscle protein
  synthesis ([Milewski et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25028798/);
  [Lamon et al. 2021](https://physoc.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.14814/phy2.14660)). A bad night
  is a cue to downshift, which is why Forge's readiness check exists.
- **Don't max the deadlift often** — reserve true 1RMs for the qualifier window plus a couple of
  rehearsals.

## 6. Event-specific methods

- **Deadlift 1RM:** 4–6+ hard sets/week on the pull through the build, then heavy singles/doubles +
  speed pulls to peak. Rehearse the **10-minute test window** with a fixed warm-up and a 3-attempt
  plan (~92–94% → ~98–100% → small PR).
- **Pull-ups / push-ups / Cindy:** built fastest by **grease-the-groove** — frequent, sub-maximal,
  *never to failure* mini-sets ([Tsatsouline method](https://johnfawkes.substack.com/p/greasing-the-groove-the-russian-military-secret-to-strength-endurance);
  [MTI mini-study](https://mtntactical.com/knowledge/mini-study-grease-the-groove-beats-density-for-push-up-pull-up-improvement/)).
  **Cindy pacing:** sub-divide every round from rep one (push-ups fail first); hold ~4–5 rounds/4 min
  and only empty the tank in the last 2–3 minutes ([Cindy strategy](https://przilla.app/wod/cindy)).
- **The mile (as a non-runner):** ~80/20 **polarized** base (mostly easy), then sharpen with
  **Norwegian 4×4** (4×4 min @ ~85–95% HRmax, 3 min jog) + one weekly mile-pace session; expect
  ~5–13% VO2max gain over 8 weeks ([Stöggl & Sperlich, polarized](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3912323/);
  [Helgerud et al. 2007](https://rcc.hslu.ch/fileadmin/user_upload/downloads/sport/Aerobic_High-Intensity_Intervals_Improve_J.Helgerud_2007.pdf);
  [the Norwegian secret](https://runningmagazine.ca/sections/training/the-norwegian-secret-to-boosting-vo2-max/)).
  Keeping the squat/deadlift in improves running economy ([Sports Med 2024 meta](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38165636/)).
- **Sandbag ladder:** drill the scoop-lap-explosive-shoulder technique to efficiency, then train
  power-endurance with **"max shoulderings in 2:00"** simulations + heavy carries for grip
  ([sandbag technique](https://liftbigeatbig.com/sandbag-carry/);
  [Dan John on carries](https://medium.com/@danjohn84123/loads-for-loaded-carries-d97366b11190)).
- **Field metcon:** local muscular endurance → lactate-tolerance intervals (30s near-max / easy) →
  full event simulations on a measured ~100 yd with a true sprint finish; pace early rounds so the
  finish is the fastest segment ([HIIT & lactate clearance](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2024.1451464/full)).
- **Grip** is a shared limiter for *two* scored events (deadlift + sandbag) — carries and dead-hangs
  appear all year.

## 7. Nutrition & recovery (the multiplier)

The basics beat the details — totals and sleep first, timing second
([ISSN protein](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5477153/);
[ISSN nutrient timing](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5596471/)):

- **Protein:** ~1.8 g/kg/day floor, up to ~2.2–2.6 g/kg in a fat-loss block; ~0.3 g/kg across 4 meals
  + a pre-sleep dose.
- **Carbohydrate, periodized to the day:** ~3–5 g/kg on lift/rest days, 6–8+ g/kg on big running/metcon
  days. Use carbs (not protein) as the fat-loss dial. Under-fueling carbs while ramping running
  *amplifies* interference.
- **Recomposition near maintenance:** −100 to −300 kcal early; never exceed ~1%/week loss; hold at
  maintenance the final 8–12 weeks so the endurance events are fueled
  ([Barakat et al., recomposition](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/343549590_Body_Recomposition_Can_Trained_Individuals_Build_Muscle_and_Lose_Fat_at_the_Same_Time)).
- **Sleep 8–9 h** is non-negotiable (see §5). **Creatine** 3–5 g/day, year-round
  ([ISSN creatine](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5469049/)).
- **Rehearse race-day fueling** (high-carb meal 2–3 h prior, caffeine 45–60 min before the mile/metcon,
  carb sips between the three back-to-back finals events) — never try it new on game day.
